The Opportunity
Why this is worth examining — and what makes RBI's position structurally different from any generic insurance startup.
The core insight: Every borrower in RBI's pipeline is legally required to carry property insurance before their loan funds. That requirement does not go away. The only question is whether the commission on that policy goes to an outside broker — or stays inside RBI.

What Kind of Insurance Are We Talking About?

RBI lends on investment properties — fix-and-flip projects, DSCR rentals, bridge loans, and new construction. These are not standard homeowner's policies. They are specialty lines that most retail agents cannot write and most online comparison platforms don't serve. This is important: the brokerage is not competing with State Farm. It is operating in the surplus lines and specialty property market where broker relationships and expertise matter more than advertising budgets.

Critically — based on RBI's publicly listed recent deals, the average loan size appears to be well over $1M, not the $200K originally assumed. Recent funded deals on their website range from approximately $1M to $4.5M+, with bridge loans, new construction, and fix-and-flip projects concentrated in South Florida, Texas, and other high-value markets. At these loan sizes, the insured property values — and therefore the insurance premiums and commissions — are materially larger than a typical residential investment property. Why Geography Matters for Revenue

Property insurance premiums vary significantly by state. High-risk markets — coastal states, catastrophe-prone regions — produce premiums 2–3x higher than the national average of approximately $2,181 for a standard residential policy. For investment properties specifically, premiums run an additional 15–25% above owner-occupied rates. This matters because broker commissions are a percentage of premium — a higher-premium state generates more revenue per policy placed, for the exact same amount of work. Until RBI's state-by-state loan volume is confirmed, this remains an assumption. The geographic mix will be one of the most important inputs to the financial model.

The national investment property insurance market has also seen meaningful new carrier entry following recent state-level legislative reforms. Carrier appetite for new agency appointments is currently stronger than it has been in several years — this is good timing for a new brokerage seeking to build a carrier panel.

The Vertical Integration Precedent

RBI has already run this playbook twice. Title services and inspection services were added as vertical revenue streams on identical logic: every closing needs them, the lender controls the customer at the point of need, and capturing the revenue internally compounds over time. Insurance is the third iteration — with one critical difference. Title and inspection are one-time fees per transaction. Insurance generates a renewal commission every year, automatically, on every policy ever placed. The book of business doesn't reset. It compounds.

How the Business Works
The mechanics of a pure brokerage model, the commission structure, how the money flows, and how it integrates with RBI's loan pipeline.

Pure Brokerage — No Underwriting Risk

RBI Insurance Brokerage LLC would operate exclusively as a licensed P&C insurance broker. It does not underwrite policies, hold insurance reserves, or assume any actuarial risk. It is an intermediary: it shops coverage across multiple carriers, presents the best options to the borrower, binds the selected policy, and earns a commission paid by the carrier — not the borrower. The borrower pays their premium directly to the carrier. The brokerage's revenue is entirely commission-based, with zero exposure to claims.

Pipeline Integration — The 5-Step Workflow

The more seamlessly the brokerage is embedded into RBI's loan workflow, the higher the capture rate and the more valuable the business becomes. The ideal setup is a system-level trigger at loan approval — borrower receives a quote as part of the standard closing checklist, clicks to bind, done. RBI already has the closing infrastructure, the tech team, and the borrower touchpoints to make this work. The integration is the mechanism that turns loan volume directly into insurance revenue.

  1. TriggerLoan approval in RBI's system notifies the brokerage with property address, loan amount, and property type. A system-level trigger at approval — rather than a manual step — maximizes how early in the process the borrower receives the quote.
  2. QuoteBrokerage runs a multi-carrier comparison in the AMS within 24–48 hours. Investment property specialty carriers only — this is not a homeowner's policy search.
  3. DeliveryClean one-page quote summary sent to borrower as part of the standard closing checklist. Borrower clicks to bind. Loan processor is copied for tracking.
  4. Bind or WaiveBorrower accepts and coverage is bound immediately, or provides their own certificate of insurance. Either way, the loan proceeds. No pressure, no friction.
  5. Certificate + Renewal TrackingCertificate of insurance issued naming RBI as loss payee/additional insured. AMS flags renewal 45 days before expiration — brokerage re-quotes if needed and earns renewal commission.
The integration is the multiplier. The deeper the brokerage is embedded into the loan workflow — automated trigger at approval, quote delivered as part of the closing checklist, certificate issued seamlessly — the higher the capture rate and the more value the brokerage generates. The good news is that RBI already has the technology team, the borrower relationships, and the closing infrastructure to make this plug-and-play. The opportunity scales directly with how tightly it's woven in.

Licensing & Compliance
The most commonly underestimated part of launching a brokerage. Getting this wrong — even by omission — creates fines, forced shutdowns, and personal liability.
This section is intentionally detailed. Read it fully before committing to a timeline. Licensing involves third-party timelines outside the brokerage's control, and compliance is an ongoing operational function — not a one-time checkbox.

The 2-20 General Lines Agent License — Where to Start

Since RBI is headquartered in Florida, the natural starting point is the Florida 2-20 General Lines (P&C) Agent License, issued by the Florida Department of Financial Services. This license covers all P&C products in Florida and — critically — enables non-resident reciprocal licensing in most other states without retaking exams. It is the standard license for independent agency principals and the most efficient path to multi-state coverage.

E&O Insurance — Non-Negotiable Before Operations

Errors & Omissions (E&O) insurance is professional liability coverage for the brokerage. It protects the entity if a borrower claims the brokerage placed wrong coverage, gave bad advice, or missed a disclosure — and sues. Most carriers will reject an appointment application from a brokerage without active E&O. It must be obtained before the first appointment application is submitted.

Cost & Coverage

New P&C agency with $1M/$1M limits: $2,500–$4,500/year. Increases as premium volume grows. This is a permanent fixed operating cost — it never goes away.

Critical: Claims-Made Policy

E&O coverage only applies if the policy is active when the claim is filed, not when the incident occurred. A coverage lapse — even briefly — creates personal liability for past placements. Tail coverage is required if the brokerage ever closes.

Multi-State Licensing — The Real Complexity

RBI operates in 40+ states. To place policies on loans outside the home state, the brokerage needs a non-resident producer license in every state where it writes business. Most states offer reciprocal licensing for 2-20 holders — no new exam required, just an application and fee. But 40 licenses means 40 renewal dates, 40 CE requirements, and 40 sets of compliance rules. This is manageable with the right system — but it requires dedicated ongoing attention, not just a one-time setup.

RESPA — Requires a Legal Opinion Before Launch

This is not a risk to dismiss. RESPA (Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act) prohibits kickbacks and unearned fees in federally related mortgage transactions when an affiliated entity receives a referral fee. Hard money loans on investment properties are generally not RESPA-covered (they are business-purpose loans, not consumer mortgages) — but this is not a determination to make without a qualified attorney. The exact loan types RBI originates, the structure of the inter-company referral arrangement, and state-specific anti-referral laws all affect the analysis. A legal opinion must be obtained before the brokerage accepts its first referral from RBI. This is a one-time cost, not ongoing — but it cannot be skipped.

Key State Regulatory Requirements

Broker Fee Caps

Most states cap retail broker fees per transaction — separate from commissions. Florida, for example, caps fees at $35 per transaction. The brokerage cannot charge borrowers additional processing or advisory fees beyond what each state permits.

Premium Trust Account

Required by law. Borrower premium payments cannot comingle with operating funds. A dedicated bank account must be established from day one, with clean accounting maintained at all times.

Mandatory Disclosure

All compensation to the brokerage — commissions, fees, contingent payments — must be disclosed to the borrower in writing. Failure to disclose is a regulatory violation, not just a best practice.

Market Conduct Examinations

State insurance departments conduct periodic audits of licensed agencies — reviewing policy files, commission disclosures, trust account records, and CE compliance. Clean records from day one are required, not aspirational.

The Time Cost — Often Underestimated

What "Running This" Actually Means

The 200-hour pre-licensing course is a real 200 hours. Exam prep on top of that is another 40–80 hours. Carrier appointments, AMS setup, pipeline integration, loan officer briefings, and processing the first wave of quotes is several hundred more hours before the operation runs smoothly. This is a full-time undertaking from the day the pipeline goes live — not a part-time effort. A brokerage run at half-attention will produce slow quotes, miss compliance deadlines, and lose borrowers — and the capture rate will reflect that.

Go-to-Market & Execution
How this actually launches, what success looks like at each phase, and how the platform expands beyond RBI's own pipeline.
Phase 1 · Months 1–6
Home State First — Internal Channel Activation

There is no external go-to-market in Phase 1. No advertising. No cold outreach. No external sales effort. The entire strategy is internal activation — getting RBI's own loan pipeline to systematically route insurance conversations to the brokerage at the moment of loan approval.

The sequence is fixed and sequential: obtain the license → form the LLC → secure E&O → appoint 3–5 carriers → configure the AMS → integrate with RBI's loan system → brief loan officers → process first quotes. Each step depends on the previous one. The realistic timeline from go-decision to first live quote is 90 days minimum — and that assumes no delays in licensing or carrier appointments, which should not be assumed.

Success metric: What percentage of RBI's borrowers are placing insurance through the brokerage? Target: 40–50% within 6 months of going live. Tracking this number monthly lets the team optimize the workflow and identify where in the closing process quotes can be delivered even earlier.

Phase 2 · Months 7–18
National Expansion — License the States, Unlock the Volume

Once the home state operation is stable, the next step is licensing in the states where RBI has the most loan volume. This is a purely administrative exercise — the same AMS, same workflow, same carriers. Just non-resident license filings in each state.

The right approach is to identify RBI's top states from actual loan data and file them simultaneously in one concerted push. Staggering state-by-state is inefficient. Each new state unlocked adds its share of RBI's loan volume to the addressable market with zero incremental infrastructure cost. The marginal cost of adding a new state is approximately $150–$300 in filing fees.

Success metric: National policies as a growing % of total book. Each state unlocked directly expands the addressable market — RBI's 40+ state footprint is untapped commission revenue waiting to be activated.

Phase 3 · Year 2–3
Platform Expansion — Other Hard Money Lenders

By Year 2–3, the brokerage will have built something valuable: a working pipeline integration, 20+ active state licenses, a trained AMS workflow, active specialty carrier appointments, and a track record with investment property insurers. That infrastructure has value beyond RBI's own borrowers.

There are hundreds of private lenders across the country doing 50–500 loans per month with the identical problem — their borrowers need insurance, outside brokers are capturing that revenue, and no one has built an embedded solution for them. RBI Insurance Brokerage can offer to become their insurance infrastructure: handle all policy placement for their borrowers, split the commission, and let the lender earn passive revenue with zero operational burden.

Each lender partnership adds volume without proportional cost increases. The fixed infrastructure — licensing, AMS, staffing — is already in place. This phase requires a dedicated outbound sales function, and should only be pursued once the RBI internal channel is operating cleanly. Selling a platform that isn't working yet is a credibility problem that is hard to recover from.

Competitive Landscape
Who the brokerage competes against, what the largest players in the hard money space are actually doing on insurance, and where the real advantage sits.

Competition for Individual Policy Placements

For any individual policy placement, the brokerage competes against whoever the borrower would have found on their own. In practice that means general retail insurance agents who may or may not specialize in investment property, and online comparison platforms like Policygenius or Insurify that are built primarily for owner-occupied residential and largely irrelevant for fix-and-flip and DSCR borrowers. The most capable competitors in the actual specialty investment property segment are brokerages like NREIG (National Real Estate Insurance Group) — a well-regarded specialist that serves real estate investors nationally and has strong carrier relationships. The critical difference: NREIG and every other specialist broker has to find and acquire their customers. RBI's brokerage has a captive pipeline of qualified buyers delivered to it automatically at the point of closing. That is not a marginal advantage. It is a structural one.

What the Real Moat Is — And What It Isn't

The Real Moat

RBI's captive loan pipeline. The brokerage's structural advantage is not its insurance expertise, its carrier relationships, or its technology — all of which can be replicated. It is the guaranteed access to hundreds of qualified, time-pressured insurance buyers per month, delivered automatically at the moment of closing. That pipeline is unique to RBI. No outside competitor can access it without building their own equivalent lending operation. The position is genuinely defensible.

What Isn't a Moat

The brokerage model itself has very low barriers to entry. Any licensed agent with E&O coverage and carrier appointments can launch one. The competitive edge comes from the distribution channel, not the insurance operation. This is actually a strength — it means the complexity and capital required to get started are minimal, and the value of the business scales with RBI's existing loan volume rather than requiring a standalone customer acquisition engine.

The Broader Signal

The fact that Kiavi ($23B funded), Lima One ($7B funded), and RCN Capital ($2.5B+ funded) have all left insurance revenue on the table is notable — and somewhat surprising given the volume they process. It likely reflects organizational inertia more than a deliberate strategic decision. None of them appear to have studied the opportunity the way this memo does. That is the window. OfferMarket has validated the concept at small scale. No one has built it at RBI's scale. The question is whether RBI moves before a well-capitalized competitor decides it's worth doing.

A Full-Time Operation From Day One

In Year 1 the brokerage is lean but fully active. On any given day: processing incoming quote requests from RBI's loan pipeline, running multi-carrier comparisons in the AMS, communicating with borrowers to bind coverage, issuing certificates of insurance naming RBI as loss payee, managing carrier relationships and appointment renewals, and tracking compliance deadlines across active state licenses.

Hard money loans close fast — sometimes with 24–48 hours of final notice. If a borrower needs insurance bound by tomorrow and the brokerage is unresponsive, the loan gets delayed or the borrower finds another broker. Speed and availability are core to the value proposition and need to be built into the operation from day one.

RBI's Built-In Advantages

Most insurance brokerages spend their first 1–2 years doing nothing but building the things RBI already has: a steady flow of qualified customers, a trusted brand, an operational closing infrastructure, a technology team, and office space. The brokerage plugs directly into all of it. The pipeline integration — connecting the loan approval trigger to the brokerage quote workflow — is the one technical piece that needs to be built. Given RBI already has title and inspection services integrated into the same pipeline, this is a solved problem operationally. The template exists.

Financial Model — To Be Completed

Building accurate projections before confirming the inputs below would produce misleading numbers. The financial model will be built as a separate deliverable once these data points are confirmed directly with RBI.

  • Current monthly loan closings (actual run rate, not 2-year average)
  • State-by-state loan volume breakdown — highest concentration states
  • Average loan size on recent deals (drives insured value + premium)
  • % of borrowers who already carry insurance at closing
  • Loan type mix — fix & flip vs. DSCR vs. bridge vs. construction
  • Average loan term length (affects renewal duration assumptions)
  • Whether RBI subsidizes office space, tech, and CRM infrastructure
  • Agreed compensation structure for the brokerage operator (salary vs. equity)
What the model will show: 3-year revenue by stream (new policies, renewals, flood, contingent commissions), full cost structure by year, monthly burn rate, breakeven analysis (policies/month needed to cover opex), capture rate sensitivity analysis showing revenue at 20%, 40%, 60%, and 80% conversion, and a startup cash requirement summary.

Questions to Answer First

These inputs materially shape the financial projections and help size the opportunity accurately. The more of these that can be confirmed, the more precise the financial model.

1
How many loans are you closing per month right now?
Need the current run rate, not the 2-year average. Loan volume is the single biggest variable in the entire revenue model.
2
What's the state breakdown — roughly what % is in which states? Top 5 states?
Higher-premium states generate 2–3x more commission per policy than lower-premium ones. Geographic concentration is one of the biggest variables in the revenue model.
3
What's the average loan size across the full book — not just the recent deals on the website?
RBI's publicly listed recent deals show loans mostly in the $1M–$4.5M range — far above the $200K originally assumed. If accurate across the full portfolio, this dramatically increases the per-policy commission. Builder's risk on a $1.5M project generates 3–5x more commission than on a $200K one. This is the most important number to nail down.
4
When borrowers come to closing, do they usually already have insurance sorted — or are they scrambling last minute?
This defines the addressable market. If most already have coverage through their own agent, the capturable pool is smaller. If most are scrambling, the conversion case is much stronger.
5
What's the loan type mix — mostly fix & flip, or a significant amount of DSCR/rental too?
Fix & flip requires builder's risk / vacant dwelling coverage. DSCR/rental requires DP-3 landlord policies. The mix determines which carrier relationships and products to prioritize from day one.
6
Does RBI currently have any informal referral arrangement with an insurance broker or carrier?
If a relationship already exists — even informally — it needs to be understood before building anything. An existing arrangement may need to be unwound or may carry obligations that affect the structure.

The Bottom Line

RBI already has everything that makes this opportunity viable — the loan volume, the borrower relationships, the brand, the closing infrastructure, and the proven playbook from title and inspection. The insurance brokerage is the next natural iteration of vertical integration, and it's the one with the most compelling long-term economics. Every policy placed this year pays a renewal commission next year, and the year after, automatically. The startup cost is minimal. No competitor at RBI's scale has built this yet. OfferMarket has proven the model works at smaller scale — the window at the top is still open.

The considerations worth keeping in mind: compliance is a real ongoing function that needs dedicated ownership from day one, the revenue ramp takes a few months to kick in, and the legal structure of the affiliate relationship needs a one-time attorney review before launch. None of these are barriers — they're just the real costs of doing it right.

The opportunity is real. The question is simply when to start.
